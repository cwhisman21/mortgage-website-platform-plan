How Texas property taxes affect mortgage payments

A useful estimate starts with the property's current taxable value, every taxing unit that serves the address, and exemptions that may apply to the new owner, not the seller's last bill alone.

Priya Bennett Snap Mortgage Editorial Contributor Snap editorial voice, not a loan officer or licensed mortgage professional.

As of Jul 13, 2026

Texas property taxes are local, address-specific costs that can change the total mortgage payment even when principal and interest stay the same.

Texas does not impose a state property tax. County appraisal districts value taxable property, while school districts, cities, counties, and other local taxing units set rates and collect revenue for local services. That division matters to a homebuyer: there is no single statewide percentage that can reliably estimate the bill for every address.

Property tax also sits outside the principal-and-interest calculation. It may be collected through an escrow account as part of the total mortgage payment, and that escrow portion can change when the tax bill changes. The state rules and exemption amounts discussed here are current as of July 13, 2026; a county appraisal district and the taxing units serving the property remain the sources for an address-specific estimate.

Key takeaways

  • Build a Texas tax estimate from the property's taxable value and the combined rates for the exact address, rather than applying a statewide average.
  • Do not assume the seller's exemptions or capped appraised value will carry over after a purchase; ask the county appraisal district how the property may be treated under new ownership.
  • The residence-homestead limitation applies to appraised value under defined conditions, not directly to the percentage increase in the final tax bill.
  • Keep property tax, homeowners insurance, association dues, and mortgage insurance visible beside principal and interest when reviewing the total monthly housing cost.

Follow the Texas property-tax chain

The appraisal district determines a property's value for the tax year. The taxable value for a particular taxing unit reflects that value after exemptions or other adjustments that apply to that unit. Each local government then applies its adopted rate. Because an address can sit within several jurisdictions, the complete bill may include separate amounts for a school district, county, city, hospital district, community college, or other special district.

For planning, collect three items: the appraisal district's current value record, the list of taxing units attached to the parcel, and each unit's adopted rate. Add the resulting unit-level estimates rather than treating one listed rate as the whole bill. The Comptroller directs owners to local appraisal districts for valuation and exemption questions and to the relevant taxing units or tax assessor-collector for rates and bills.

Separate homestead exemptions from value limits

A qualifying residence homestead may receive exemptions that remove part of its appraised value from taxation. As of July 13, 2026, the Texas Comptroller states that school districts must provide a $140,000 general residence-homestead exemption. Some local taxing units may offer additional exemptions, and the appraisal district decides whether an application meets the applicable requirements. A buyer should confirm eligibility and filing steps instead of subtracting an exemption before it has been granted.

Texas also limits annual increases in the appraised value of a residence homestead when the owner qualified for the exemption in the preceding and current year. The limit generally takes effect on January 1 of the tax year after qualification and does not include the market value of qualifying new improvements in the same way. This is a limit on appraised value, not a promise that the tax bill or escrow deposit cannot rise by more than 10 percent; exemptions, adopted rates, taxing-unit boundaries, and other bill components can change.

Rebuild the estimate for the buyer's ownership

The seller's tax bill can be useful history, but it may reflect exemptions, ownership dates, or an appraised-value limitation that will not apply to the buyer in the same way. Ask the appraisal district for the current record and how a change in ownership may affect the next appraisal cycle. Then request the latest adopted rates from the taxing units serving the parcel. This approach is more defensible than multiplying the purchase price by the prior owner's effective tax percentage.

Leave room for timing differences. A tax estimate used during the loan process may precede a later appraisal notice, exemption decision, or adopted rate. Review the Loan Estimate and Closing Disclosure to see which property costs are included in projected payments and escrow. If a cost is not escrowed, plan for the bill separately instead of treating its absence from the mortgage payment as a savings.

Plan for tax changes after closing

An escrow account spreads expected tax and insurance costs across monthly mortgage payments, but it does not freeze those costs. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau explains that a change in taxes or insurance can change the escrow payment and the total amount sent to the servicer. An annual escrow statement can show projected disbursements, shortages, surpluses, and the resulting payment adjustment.

Keep the appraisal notice, exemption confirmation, tax statement, insurance renewal, and escrow analysis together. If the servicer's projection appears inconsistent with a new bill, contact the servicer and compare the underlying documents. For future purchase planning, test a higher tax assumption as a cushion, but label it as a scenario rather than an expected bill.

Conclusion

A Texas property-tax estimate becomes more useful when every input has an owner and a date. The appraisal district supplies the value and exemption record, local taxing units supply rates, and the servicer explains how the resulting bill is handled in escrow. Recheck those inputs before closing and after each annual notice so the total housing budget does not depend on a seller's tax history continuing unchanged.

FAQs

Is there one Texas property-tax rate I can use for every home?

No. Texas property taxes are imposed by local taxing units, and the combination of jurisdictions, rates, taxable value, and exemptions depends on the property. Use the exact parcel and current local records for an estimate.

Does the 10 percent homestead limit cap my tax bill increase at 10 percent?

No. The state guidance describes a limit on annual growth in a qualifying residence homestead's appraised value. The final bill can also reflect adopted rates, exemptions, new improvements, and other changes.

Will a fixed-rate mortgage keep the escrow portion of my payment fixed?

Not necessarily. Principal and interest may remain level on a standard fixed-rate loan, but property taxes and insurance premiums can change. If they are escrowed, the total mortgage payment may change after an escrow analysis.
