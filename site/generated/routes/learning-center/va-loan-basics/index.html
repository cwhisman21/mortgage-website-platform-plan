VA loan basics for eligible Veterans, service members, and surviving spouses

Start with the benefit record, then compare the actual loan. A COE documents VA home-loan eligibility based on service history; it does not promise a loan amount, rate, or approval.

Marcus Lane Snap Mortgage Editorial Contributor Snap editorial voice, not a loan officer or licensed mortgage professional.

As of Jul 13, 2026

A VA-backed loan can offer a distinct purchase path for eligible Veterans, service members, and some surviving spouses, but the Certificate of Eligibility, entitlement, lender review, property value, funding fee, and closing costs each answer a different question.

VA-backed purchase loans are issued by private lenders, while the Department of Veterans Affairs guarantees part of the loan. The first program step is usually confirming whether the borrower can receive a Certificate of Eligibility. The COE shows service-based benefit information and entitlement, but the lender separately reviews credit, income, debts, assets, occupancy, and the requested loan amount.

The program may permit a purchase without a down payment when the transaction, entitlement, appraisal, and lender review support that structure. That feature should be evaluated beside the VA funding fee, other closing costs, the amount financed, cash retained after closing, and the full monthly housing budget.

Key takeaways

  • A Certificate of Eligibility confirms service-related VA home-loan benefit information; it does not replace the lender's underwriting decision.
  • A no-down-payment option may be available, but entitlement, appraised value, purchase price, lender standards, and the proposed loan all matter.
  • VA-backed loans do not require monthly mortgage insurance, while a one-time funding fee may apply unless the borrower has a confirmed exemption.
  • Interest rates, points, and many closing costs come from the private lender, so compare written offers rather than treating VA as a single price.

Separate the COE from loan approval

VA determines whether the service history and duty status support a COE. A borrower can request the certificate directly or may ask a lender to help retrieve it. Because eligibility rules vary by service period and circumstance, use the VA's current guidance rather than relying on a general service-length shortcut.

After the COE is available, the lender evaluates the financing request. The review may include documented income, debts, credit history, assets, occupancy, and the property's appraised value. Full entitlement does not mean an unlimited approval; it means the county loan limit does not cap the guaranty calculation in the same way it can when some entitlement is already in use.

Read entitlement, down payment, and value together

For a borrower with full entitlement, VA states that there is no home-loan limit as long as the borrower can afford the amount and the appraisal supports the purchase price. When entitlement is already in use, the county's FHFA conforming limit may affect the guaranty calculation, and a lender may require a down payment if remaining entitlement is not enough for the proposed loan.

A purchase price above the appraised value can create another cash decision. The buyer may discuss a reconsideration of value through the lender, renegotiate the contract, use an available contract right, or pay a difference in cash if the transaction can otherwise proceed. Those choices depend on the contract, available funds, and lender review.

Account for the funding fee and closing costs

The VA funding fee is generally a one-time program charge, but exemptions apply in defined circumstances. Its amount can depend on the loan type, loan amount, whether the benefit has been used before, and, for some transactions, the down payment. Confirm the borrower's status on current VA records rather than assuming the fee applies or is waived.

When permitted, financing the funding fee reduces the amount due at closing but increases the loan balance and interest paid over time. Other purchase closing costs are handled separately. Review the Loan Estimate for lender charges, discount points, appraisal, title, recording, taxes, insurance, prepaids, escrow funding, seller credits, and the final estimate of cash to close.

Plan for the appraisal, inspection, and closing

A VA-approved appraiser provides an opinion of value and reviews the home against VA minimum property requirements. VA explains that the appraisal is not the same as a home inspection. A buyer can consider arranging an independent inspection to examine condition and maintenance concerns, subject to the purchase contract and local practice.

Ask the lender about appraisal timing, condominium approval when relevant, required repairs, and the documents still needed for underwriting. Before closing, compare the final Closing Disclosure with the selected Loan Estimate and resolve unexpected changes in the loan amount, rate, fees, credits, funding fee, or cash required.

Conclusion

A VA-backed loan is best understood as a sequence, not a single benefit claim. Confirm the COE and entitlement, let the lender review the borrower and property, then compare the proposed rate, fee treatment, payment, and cash to close with other available options. That approach preserves the value of the program without assuming that every eligible borrower should use the same structure.

FAQs

Does a VA Certificate of Eligibility guarantee loan approval?

No. A COE confirms service-based benefit information. The private lender still reviews credit, income, debts, assets, occupancy, the property, and the requested amount.

Does every VA purchase loan require no down payment?

No single structure applies to every transaction. Entitlement, appraised value, purchase price, lender standards, and the borrower's selected terms can affect whether cash is required.

Is the VA funding fee always charged?

No. VA identifies exemptions for certain borrowers and circumstances. Confirm the current funding-fee status before closing and review how any fee is shown on the Loan Estimate.

Can the VA home-loan benefit be used again?

It may be reusable through restored or remaining entitlement. The result depends on the borrower's COE, prior VA-backed loans, property disposition, and current VA requirements.
