Mortgage guide

Home Equity

Compare ways to borrow against home equity without losing sight of the house as collateral, the existing first mortgage, changing payments, fees, and total repayment cost.

Covered by Elena Park

Overview

A HELOC, home equity loan, and cash-out refinance can all turn equity into borrowed funds, but they do it differently. The rate structure, draw access, repayment schedule, closing costs, lien position, and effect on the existing mortgage shape which questions matter.

Begin with the amount and timing of the need, then compare only realistic scenarios. Equity estimates can change with valuation, and available terms depend on the property, existing liens, borrower finances, lender requirements, and market conditions.

Estimate equity conservatively by comparing a supportable property value with every loan or lien secured by the home. The amount a lender will permit you to borrow may be lower than the mathematical equity because combined loan-to-value limits, credit, income, debts, occupancy, property type, and product rules apply. A valuation can also differ from an online estimate. Ask which value and balance assumptions are preliminary, whether an appraisal or automated valuation will be used, and how a lower value would change proceeds and costs.

Match the product to the way the funds will be used and repaid. A HELOC may support expenses that arrive in stages, but the rate is often variable and payments can change when the draw period ends. A home equity loan can provide a lump sum with a defined schedule, while leaving the first mortgage in place. A cash-out refinance replaces the first mortgage and applies new terms to the entire balance. Compare each option over the same expected holding period, including fees, minimum payments, rate changes, and total balance reduction.

Treat every option as debt secured by the home. Build a repayment plan that does not depend on uncertain appreciation, a future refinance, or income that has not arrived. Consider alternatives such as saving in stages, an unsecured loan, contractor financing, selling another asset, or reducing the project scope, with attention to their own risks and costs. Tax treatment can depend on how proceeds are used and individual circumstances, so consult a qualified tax adviser rather than assuming interest is deductible. Preserve reserves and understand the consequences of missed payments before borrowing.

Why it matters

Equity is not the same as spendable cash. Using it creates debt secured by the home, so payment volatility, repayment timing, fees, and the consequences of replacing a favorable first mortgage deserve close attention.

What to compare

HELOC

Reusable access may fit staged expenses, but variable rates and draw-to-repayment changes can make future payments less predictable.

Home equity loan

A separate lump-sum loan may offer a defined repayment schedule while adding another payment and lien.

Cash-out refinance

One new first mortgage can simplify the loan structure but changes the rate, term, costs, and payment on the full refinanced balance.
