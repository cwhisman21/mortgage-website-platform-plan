Taxes & Insurance

Estimate property taxes and homeowners insurance with the right jurisdiction, property details, and source date so the non-loan part of the payment is not an afterthought.

Covered by Maya Brooks, Priya Bennett

Overview

Taxes and insurance can materially change the cost of owning two similarly priced homes. Tax assessments, exemptions, local levies, coverage needs, deductibles, insurer pricing, flood or hazard exposure, and association requirements can all be property-specific.

Use broad estimates to screen a budget, then replace them with information from the relevant tax authority, insurance professionals, property records, and transaction documents. An escrow account collects certain costs with the mortgage payment; it does not fix the underlying bill.

For property taxes, identify every relevant jurisdiction and ask how assessed value is established after a transfer. The seller's current bill may reflect an older assessment, exemptions you will not receive, caps, or a partial-year amount. Review county or municipal records, but note their effective date and whether pending levies or special assessments appear elsewhere. If an exemption may apply, confirm eligibility, filing steps, and timing with the responsible authority. Convert a reasonable annual estimate to a monthly amount while retaining a cushion for reassessment or rate changes.

For insurance, request property-specific quotes early enough to compare coverage, not just premiums. Construction type, age, roof, prior claims, location, replacement cost, liability limits, deductibles, wildfire, wind, flood, earthquake, and other hazards may affect availability and price. A standard homeowners policy does not cover every risk, and a lender's minimum requirements may not equal the protection a household wants. Ask an appropriately licensed insurance professional what is included, excluded, limited, or handled by a separate policy, and update the quote if inspection findings change.

Escrow can make annual bills easier to budget by collecting funds with the mortgage payment, but shortages or surpluses can arise when bills or projections change. Review the initial escrow deposit, prepaid items, payment schedule, and later escrow analyses. Keep association dues and assessments separate unless the loan documentation expressly handles them. Before making an offer or choosing between homes, compare the combined principal, interest, taxes, insurance, mortgage insurance, dues, and a maintenance allowance. Recheck the figures before closing and maintain room for future increases after becoming the owner.

Build a routine for checking these costs after closing. Read tax notices and escrow analyses instead of assuming the servicer has corrected every change, and keep insurance contact information current. Before renewing a policy, compare the dwelling limit, loss-settlement basis, deductibles, exclusions, and separate hazard coverage, not the premium alone. Major improvements, a new roof, or an occupancy change can be reasons to ask whether coverage still fits. If a bill changes, separate the underlying tax or premium change from the servicer's escrow collection so you know which party can explain or correct it.

Why it matters

A principal-and-interest estimate can look affordable while leaving out ownership costs that change by address and over time. Checking them early produces a more durable payment range and fewer surprises before closing.
