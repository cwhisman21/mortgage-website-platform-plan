VA Loans

See how VA-backed financing connects eligibility, entitlement, the funding fee, property standards, and lender review for an eligible service member, veteran, or surviving spouse.

Covered by Marcus Lane

Last updated July 13, 2026. Topic guidance reviewed through this date. Time-sensitive figures show their own as-of dates.

Overview

The VA home loan benefit can support purchase or refinance financing through private lenders, subject to VA rules and lender review. Eligibility and entitlement are important starting points, but the property, occupancy, income, debts, credit, and loan terms still matter.

A useful VA comparison looks beyond the down payment. Review the funding fee or any applicable exemption, closing costs, rate and APR, seller concessions, property process, and how much cash you want to keep after closing.

Start by obtaining or confirming the Certificate of Eligibility and discussing available entitlement with a VA-experienced lender. Prior use of the benefit, an existing VA loan, restoration of entitlement, county loan limits in certain entitlement situations, and the intended occupancy can affect the structure. Eligibility for the benefit does not replace underwriting. The lender still evaluates documented income, debts, credit history, residual-income considerations, assets, and the proposed property before issuing an approval or final terms.

Review the VA funding fee carefully. The amount can vary with the transaction, down payment, and prior benefit use, while eligible borrowers may have a documented exemption. Ask the lender to verify the applicable treatment and show the effect of financing the fee versus paying it at closing. Then compare rate, APR, lender charges, third-party costs, prepaid items, discount points, credits, and retained reserves. No required down payment in one eligible scenario does not mean there will be no cash needed or that making a down payment is never useful.

Connect financing with the offer and property process. VA appraisal and minimum property requirements are distinct from a comprehensive home inspection, and the appraisal does not guarantee a property's condition. Contract provisions, seller-paid costs, concessions, appraisal timing, repairs, and local competition require coordinated planning with qualified professionals. Avoid assuming a VA offer is inherently weak or automatically accepted. The best approach is a well-documented financing plan, a realistic timeline, and clear communication about the buyer's verified position while preserving the protections and flexibility the borrower values.

Because private lenders provide VA-backed financing, the benefit does not create one universal rate or fee package. Request written estimates based on the same loan amount, property, down payment, term, and lock period. Compare APR, points, credits, lender charges, funding-fee treatment, projected payments, and cash to close. Ask which estimates depend on facts that have not yet been verified and what may change after appraisal or underwriting. Keep the full monthly ownership cost and post-closing reserves in view; preserving cash can be useful, but a larger balance also affects the payment and long-term cost.

Why it matters

A strong benefit can still be misunderstood when entitlement, fees, property requirements, and lender overlays are collapsed into one promise. Separating those pieces helps borrowers ask better questions without assuming approval or a particular term.
