Buying a Home

Build a purchase plan around the full monthly cost, cash needed at closing, and the steps between early budgeting and a property-specific loan review.

Covered by Jordan Avery

Last updated July 13, 2026. Topic guidance reviewed through this date. Time-sensitive figures show their own as-of dates.

Overview

A comfortable home price starts with more than principal and interest. Property taxes, homeowners insurance, mortgage insurance when applicable, association dues, maintenance, closing costs, and reserves can all affect the amount you are ready to spend.

Move in sequence: set a budget range, compare loan paths, organize documents, study the local market, and revisit the numbers when a real property enters the picture. Early estimates are planning tools, not final loan terms.

Begin with the monthly amount you can sustain while continuing to save and handle ordinary surprises. A lender's qualification calculation and your personal comfort limit answer different questions. List recurring debts, childcare, transportation, health costs, retirement contributions, and goals that may not appear in a mortgage application. Add a repair and maintenance cushion appropriate for the home's age and condition. When you test affordability, change one assumption at a time so you can see whether the price, rate, taxes, insurance, mortgage insurance, or association dues creates the greatest pressure.

Plan cash in separate buckets. The down payment is only one use of funds; closing costs, prepaid interest, initial tax and insurance deposits, inspection and appraisal costs, moving expenses, immediate repairs, and an emergency reserve may arrive close together. A larger down payment can reduce the loan or mortgage-insurance cost, but using every available dollar can leave a new homeowner exposed. Compare several down-payment choices with the same home price and keep the amount you want available after closing visible throughout the search.

When a property enters the picture, replace broad assumptions with address-specific facts. Review disclosures and inspection findings, verify association rules and fees, request an insurance quote, and examine available tax information without assuming the seller's bill will continue unchanged. Compare the Loan Estimate for each realistic financing option using rate, APR, projected payments, cash to close, lender credits, points, and loan features. Before signing, revisit the budget under a less favorable scenario. A sound purchase plan should leave room for ownership, not merely reach the closing table.

Keep the purchase timeline tied to decisions you can monitor. Ask when a rate can be locked, how long the lock lasts, which financial documents may need an update, and which contract dates depend on financing. Preapproval is not final approval; the lender must still review current borrower information and the selected property. Keep inspection, appraisal, title, insurance, and financing questions moving together so an unresolved item does not surprise the budget or closing date. Before opening new credit, moving substantial funds, changing employment, or making a major purchase, ask the loan team how that change could affect the review.

Why it matters

Purchase decisions combine a long-term loan with an immediate cash commitment and a property that has its own costs. A clear sequence helps you avoid using the listing price or headline rate as the whole affordability answer.

What to compare

Monthly comfort

Compare the full estimated housing payment with the room you want for savings, repairs, and other priorities.

Upfront cash

Review down payment, closing costs, prepaid taxes and insurance, deposits, and the reserves you want to keep.

Loan path

Compare program rules, mortgage insurance, rate and APR, term, property fit, and documentation rather than choosing on one feature.
