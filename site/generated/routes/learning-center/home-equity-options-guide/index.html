Home equity guide: HELOC and refinance options to compare

Equity is not the same as available cash. Each option uses the home as collateral, and the most useful comparison keeps the existing first mortgage, future borrowing needs, and exit plan in view.

Elena Park Snap Mortgage Editorial Contributor Snap editorial voice, not a loan officer or licensed mortgage professional.

As of Jul 13, 2026

Homeowners can compare a home equity loan, HELOC, and cash-out refinance by matching the way funds arrive with payment risk, lien position, fees, and the plan for repayment.

Home equity is the estimated property value minus debts secured by the home. That estimate can help frame a conversation, but it does not establish the amount a lender may offer. A lender may review the property value, existing liens, income, credit, occupancy, loan purpose, and product rules before determining whether an option is available and on what terms.

The products also solve different cash-flow problems. A home equity loan generally provides one amount at closing. A HELOC allows repeated draws up to an available line during a defined period, usually with an adjustable rate. A cash-out refinance replaces the current first mortgage with a larger new mortgage and returns part of the proceeds after payoffs and costs. Comparing only the advertised rate or first payment can hide the larger tradeoff.

Key takeaways

  • Estimate equity by subtracting all home-secured debt from a supportable property value, then expect the lender's available amount to be lower than that gross figure.
  • Match a known one-time expense, a staged or uncertain project, and a replacement-first-mortgage decision with the funding structure that actually fits the need.
  • For a HELOC, review the index, margin, caps, draw rules, fees, repayment period, and payment at a higher balance and rate.
  • Because each option is secured by the home, compare repayment capacity, total cost, future sale or refinance plans, and alternatives that do not place the property at risk.

Measure the equity estimate and define the borrowing need

Start with a dated, supportable property-value range rather than the highest automated estimate you can find. Subtract the first-mortgage payoff and every other lien secured by the property. The result is estimated gross equity, not an offer and not a recommended borrowing amount. The lender's valuation and combined lien limits may produce less available credit, and closing costs or required payoffs can reduce the cash received.

Write down the purpose, amount, timing, and repayment source before comparing products. A fixed contractor bid due at once presents a different need from a renovation paid in stages. A temporary liquidity need differs from debt that may remain for many years. If the household is already struggling with the mortgage, CFPB guidance recommends discussing alternatives with a housing counselor before adding home-secured debt.

Compare how each equity option changes the household balance sheet

A home equity loan or HELOC generally sits beside an existing first mortgage, so the original mortgage terms usually remain while a second payment and lien are added. A cash-out refinance pays off the first mortgage and creates a new first-lien balance. That distinction matters when the current mortgage has terms the homeowner values, because replacing the entire balance can change the rate, term, payment, and total interest on money that was already borrowed.

Use the same planned amount and holding period for every option. Record upfront charges, annual or transaction fees, rate structure, minimum draws, scheduled payment, possible payment change, and payoff conditions. Also compare a smaller borrowing amount, delayed project, cash contribution, or non-home-secured alternative. The home should not become collateral for an expense merely because equity appears available.

Stress-test the payment and the continued access to funds

For a HELOC, calculate a payment at the expected draw and another at the full planned balance under a higher-rate assumption. Ask whether draw-period payments include principal, when the repayment period begins, how its payment is calculated, and whether a balloon amount could be due. CFPB explains that HELOC rates are commonly variable and that payments can rise when the draw period ends. A credit line may also be reduced or frozen under conditions described in the agreement, so it should not be treated as an unconditional emergency fund.

For a home equity loan, confirm whether the rate is fixed or adjustable and whether the scheduled payment fully repays the balance by the end of the term. For cash-out refinancing, compare the proposed principal and interest with the existing mortgage on the same remaining horizon, then add taxes, insurance, and all transaction charges. A lower rate on one product does not settle the comparison when it applies to a different balance, term, or fee structure.

Review the lien, exit plan, and documents before proceeding

Any of these mortgage products can put the home at risk if required payments are not made. Read the disclosures for APR, fees, rate adjustments, payment rules, late charges, early termination terms, and the right to cancel when applicable. For a HELOC, CFPB's comparison booklet specifically directs borrowers to examine the draw and repayment periods, transaction minimums, index and margin, caps, annual charges, and possible balloon payment.

Plan the exit before opening the account. A sale generally requires liens to be paid from closing proceeds. A later first-mortgage refinance may require the HELOC lender to subordinate its lien, and CFPB notes that a lender may decline, which can require the line to be paid off. If the expected repayment source is a future sale, bonus, refinance, or investment return, test what happens when that event is later or smaller than expected.

Conclusion

The amount of equity shown on a worksheet is not a reason to borrow it. Start with the expense and repayment plan, preserve the terms of a valuable first mortgage unless replacing them survives a full-cost comparison, and model the payment after introductory or draw-period conditions end. Written disclosures should answer how the rate, fees, access, lien, and payoff work. A sound choice is the option the household can repay under a less favorable scenario, not simply the one that makes cash available fastest.

FAQs

How much of my estimated home equity can I borrow?

There is no universal amount. A lender may consider the verified property value, current mortgage and other liens, product limits, income, credit, occupancy, and purpose. Closing costs and required payoffs can further reduce net proceeds.

Can a HELOC payment change after I open the line?

Yes. HELOCs commonly use variable rates, the balance can change as you draw or repay, and the payment method may change when the draw period ends. Review the index, margin, caps, minimum payment, repayment schedule, and any balloon feature.

Will a HELOC affect a future refinance of my first mortgage?

It may. The HELOC lender may need to approve keeping its lien behind a new first mortgage. If it does not agree, the line may need to be paid off as part of the refinance.
