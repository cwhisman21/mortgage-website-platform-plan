Jumbo Loans

Prepare a jumbo mortgage file for lender-specific review

A jumbo loan is generally a conventional mortgage above the applicable conforming loan limit. Jumbo products are less standardized across lenders, so documentation, reserves, appraisal practices, property eligibility, and pricing can vary. Borrowers benefit from comparing complete written offers rather than relying on a generic threshold or checklist.

Use this Jumbo Loans guide to organize the borrower, property, payment, cash, and timing questions that belong in a written comparison. Program availability and terms depend on the complete scenario and lender review.

Loan size

Use the current county and unit-count limit

FHFA updates conforming loan limits annually and publishes higher amounts for designated high-cost areas. Determine the property's county, number of units, and proposed loan amount before labeling a transaction jumbo. The purchase price alone is not the test because the down payment affects the amount financed. Use the limit for the correct calendar year and location.

Crossing the conforming boundary does not describe one universal jumbo product. Banks, credit unions, mortgage companies, and investors can use different credit, income, asset, reserve, occupancy, and property standards. Ask each lender which rules apply to the proposed scenario and whether another down payment amount changes the product category or pricing.

Documentation

Build a clear record of income, assets, and reserves

A jumbo review may require detailed records for salary, bonuses, commissions, business income, investments, retirement distributions, trusts, multiple properties, or other sources. Requirements vary. Organize complete statements and tax or business documents requested by the lender, identify large or unusual transactions, and keep a clear trail for funds used at closing.

Reserves are assets remaining after closing that meet the lender's rules. Required amounts and eligible asset types can differ by loan, property, occupancy, and lender. Do not assume that every investment or retirement balance receives full credit. Ask how reserves are calculated, whether funds must be liquidated, and how planned closing costs, down payment, and other financed properties affect the calculation.

Property review

Leave time for valuation and property-specific questions

Higher-value and unusual properties can require more appraisal analysis, additional valuations, or specialized review. Condominiums, cooperatives, mixed-use features, acreage, unique construction, multiple units, or recent renovations may affect lender acceptance. Raise these facts before relying on a closing schedule, and ask what appraisal process the quoted product expects.

Insurance coverage and cost deserve early attention, especially in coastal, wildfire, flood, or other higher-risk markets. The lender may have coverage requirements, while the borrower's ownership budget should also consider deductibles, exclusions, association master policies, taxes, maintenance, and major repairs. A manageable principal-and-interest payment can still sit inside an unsustainable total property cost.

Offer comparison

Compare liquidity, pricing, and flexibility together

Request Loan Estimates on matching assumptions where possible: loan amount, property, occupancy, term, lock period, and closing date. Compare interest rate, APR, points, lender credits, deposits, cash to close, prepayment terms, reserve expectations, and whether the lender requires a particular account relationship. Verify any relationship benefit and its ongoing conditions before assigning value to it.

Test more than one down payment and loan-size structure. Bringing the amount below a conforming boundary may use substantial cash, while remaining jumbo may change pricing or documentation. Neither answer is automatically preferable. Preserve enough liquidity for ownership and household needs, and avoid a plan that depends on a future refinance, asset sale, or income event arriving exactly on schedule.

Questions to settle before you choose

Is a jumbo loan defined by the home price?

No. It is generally based on the loan amount relative to the applicable conforming limit for the location and unit count.

Do all jumbo lenders use the same reserve rules?

No. Documentation, reserve, property, and underwriting standards can vary, so compare the actual requirements of each offer.
