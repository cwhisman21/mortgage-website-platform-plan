Conventional Loans

Compare conventional financing through limits, insurance, and loan-level costs

Conventional mortgages are not insured or guaranteed by FHA, VA, or USDA. Some conform to standards used by Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac, while others do not. A productive comparison focuses on the applicable loan limit, borrower and property review, mortgage-insurance treatment, pricing, and total cost.

Use this Conventional Loans guide to organize the borrower, property, payment, cash, and timing questions that belong in a written comparison. Program availability and terms depend on the complete scenario and lender review.

Structure

Identify whether the proposed loan is conforming

The Federal Housing Finance Agency publishes annual conforming loan limits, including higher limits for designated high-cost areas and different amounts for certain multi-unit properties. Compare the requested loan amount with the current limit for the property's county and unit count. A purchase price alone does not determine conforming status because the down payment changes the loan amount.

Being within the limit does not establish approval or price. The lender reviews income, credit, debts, assets, occupancy, property type, value, and other factors under the program and its own requirements. Ask which program or investor framework the quote uses, especially for condominiums, manufactured homes, investment properties, multiple units, or income that needs specialized documentation.

Mortgage insurance

Understand when private mortgage insurance may apply

A conventional loan with a smaller down payment may require private mortgage insurance. Cost and availability can depend on the transaction, credit profile, loan-to-value ratio, occupancy, property, and insurer. The premium may be structured in different ways. Ask the lender to identify the method, monthly impact, upfront amounts, and any conditions relevant to cancellation or automatic termination.

Do not compare a conventional quote with FHA or another path using interest rate alone. Place rate, APR, points, lender credits, mortgage insurance, cash to close, balance, and projected costs beside each other for the same property and timeframe. A conventional option with a higher nominal rate can have a different total-cost result, while a low advertised rate may include points or assumptions that do not match the borrower.

Pricing

Connect pricing adjustments with the complete borrower scenario

Conventional pricing can vary with credit profile, loan-to-value ratio, occupancy, property type, loan purpose, loan amount, term, and other features. That does not mean any one borrower will receive a particular adjustment or quote. Obtain a Loan Estimate and ask the lender to explain points, credits, lock period, and significant assumptions in plain language.

Decide whether paying points fits how long you may keep the loan. Upfront cost can take time to recover through a lower payment, and selling or refinancing earlier can change the result. A lender credit can reduce cash due while increasing the rate or changing other economics. Compare more than one structure using the same lock period and expected closing date.

Decision

Balance down payment, reserves, and ownership plans

A larger down payment may reduce the balance and could affect pricing or insurance, but it also uses cash that might support emergencies, repairs, or other goals. A smaller down payment may preserve reserves while increasing the financed amount and monthly obligation. Model several contributions and include taxes, homeowners insurance, association dues, and maintenance in each monthly budget.

If mortgage-insurance cancellation or future refinancing is part of the plan, confirm current rules but do not treat the future outcome as certain. Property value, payment history, equity, loan terms, and applicable law or servicing requirements can matter. Choose a structure that works under today's disclosed terms and keeps enough post-closing liquidity for the property and household.

Questions to settle before you choose

Is every conventional loan conforming?

No. Conforming loans fall within applicable limits and program standards; other conventional loans may use different lender or investor requirements.

Can private mortgage insurance always be removed on a specific date?

No. Cancellation and termination depend on the loan, equity, payment history, value, applicable requirements, and servicing review.
