Measure a refinance against the mortgage you already have

Refinancing replaces an existing mortgage with a new loan. The decision should begin with a defined goal and a side-by-side comparison of the current loan and proposed loan, including closing costs, term, payment, total interest, and the time required to recover upfront expense.

Goal

Name the result you want before requesting quotes

Common refinance goals include changing the rate structure, reducing a monthly obligation, shortening or extending the term, removing mortgage insurance when the requirements are met, changing borrowers on the loan, or accessing equity. Each goal requires a different measurement. A payment reduction alone does not show whether the new loan improves the household's long-term position.

Write down the current principal balance, interest rate, remaining term, monthly principal and interest, mortgage-insurance amount, escrow charges, and any prepayment terms. Also note how long you expect to keep the property and loan. This baseline prevents a new quote from being compared with memory or with a payment that includes different tax and insurance assumptions.

Cost comparison

Calculate recovery time and the effect of restarting the term

A refinance may include lender charges, points, appraisal, title services, recording, taxes, prepaid interest, and new escrow funding. Some costs may be paid at closing, added to the balance when permitted, or offset with lender credits tied to pricing. Ask for written Loan Estimates and identify which amounts are loan costs, which are other transaction costs, and which are prepaid or escrow funds rather than fees.

A simple recovery estimate divides relevant refinance costs by expected monthly savings, but that calculation is only a starting point. It does not capture a larger balance, a longer repayment period, lost investment opportunity, tax considerations, or a future sale. Compare the balance and projected interest over the period you expect to keep the new loan, not merely the first payment.

Qualification

Expect a new review of the borrower and property

The lender generally evaluates credit, income, employment, assets, debts, occupancy, property type, value, equity, and loan purpose under the selected program. An appraisal or another accepted valuation method may be required. Existing liens, a home equity line, condominium matters, title issues, insurance availability, or recent financial changes can affect timing and available structures.

Do not assume that a quoted rate is locked or that an application is approved. Ask how long a lock lasts, what conditions apply, what happens if closing is delayed, and whether paying points fits the expected holding period. Continue making payments on the existing mortgage until the servicer confirms the payoff and transfer process. Avoid financial changes that could alter underwriting without first discussing them with the loan team.

Tradeoffs

Test the new loan under more than one time horizon

A longer term can reduce the required payment while increasing the time debt remains outstanding and potentially increasing total interest. A shorter term can accelerate payoff but require a higher payment. An adjustable structure may begin differently from a fixed-rate loan but can change according to its index, margin, caps, and adjustment schedule. Compare structures using the same balance and realistic holding periods.

If the proposal depends on a future refinance, sale, or increase in income, test what happens if that event is delayed. Review how taxes and insurance are handled, whether an escrow refund from the old servicer arrives after new escrow funds are due, and how much cash remains after closing. The stronger choice is the one that advances the stated goal without hiding costs in a larger balance or longer timeline.

Questions to settle before you choose

Does a lower refinance payment always save money?

No. The payment can fall because the term is extended or costs are added to the balance. Compare total cost, recovery time, balance, and the period you expect to keep the loan.

Can I count on refinancing again later?

No. Future rates, property value, equity, income, credit, program rules, and lender requirements can change.
