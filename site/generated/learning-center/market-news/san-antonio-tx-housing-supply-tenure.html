San Antonio, TX housing supply, ownership, and rent

What the latest ACS housing mix can and cannot tell buyers and renters comparing options in San Antonio, TX.

Maya Brooks Snap Mortgage Editorial Contributor Last updated July 10, 2026 Snap editorial voice, not a loan officer or licensed mortgage professional.

U.S. Census Bureau

Key takeaways

  • As of: 2024 ACS 5-year estimates. Source period: 2024.
  • total housing units: 612,901.
  • occupied housing units: 554,581.
  • vacant housing units: 58,320.

The dated finding: the size and use of the local housing stock

The published evidence provides the starting point: San Antonio, TX's current ACS housing baseline includes 612,901 total housing units, 554,581 occupied units, and 58,320 vacant units. Those counts can help a borrower establish the scale of the measured housing base before comparing tenure, rent, current listings, property condition, and financing. The figures describe stock across the survey period; they do not say that 58,320 homes were listed for sale or available to rent at one moment. For borrower planning, reading 612,901 total units with 554,581 occupied and 58,320 vacant units keeps the release internally grounded while leaving live availability to current listing and property evidence.

What the comparison adds: vacancy changed, but it is not listing inventory

Vacant units in San Antonio, TX increased from 47,073 in the prior ACS period to 58,320 in the current release, a reported movement of 23.9%. In the current housing stock, 58,320 units equal 9.5% of the 612,901 total. ACS vacancy covers several statuses, so the 9.5% share cannot be read as the share of homes a buyer can purchase today or the share of rentals immediately available. For the decision at hand, the movement from 47,073 to 58,320 can still prompt better questions about current for-sale and rental inventory in San Antonio, TX, provided those questions are answered with timely listing and property data in place of the survey count alone.

Planning context: ownership and renting within occupied housing

Among 554,581 occupied units in San Antonio, TX, the ACS-derived owner share is 52.2% and the renter share is 47.8%, making owner-occupied housing the larger measured tenure group. For borrower planning, the 52.2% and 47.8% figures describe how occupied units were distributed; they do not measure how many households are ready to buy, how many owners plan to sell, or which properties can qualify for a mortgage.

This local tenure mix helps a borrower avoid assuming that one housing path dominates every segment of San Antonio, TX. The 52.2% and 47.8% mix is most useful when paired with actual choices at a similar property type, location, condition, and time horizon instead of used as a signal to buy or rent by itself.

Borrower view: use rent as a matched comparison, not a shortcut

San Antonio, TX's ACS median gross rent is $1,324, while renter-occupied units account for 47.8% of occupied housing and owner-occupied units account for 52.2%. In practical terms, the $1,324 median includes rent and certain utilities for the measured rental stock; it does not identify a lease offer comparable to a home under consideration.

Comparing $1,324 with only a mortgage's principal and interest would omit taxes, insurance, maintenance, association charges, transaction costs, and differences between the properties. For the decision at hand, a more useful San Antonio, TX comparison matches real lease and ownership choices over the same expected horizon, then treats the tenure shares as market context instead of a break-even calculation.

What the housing-stock evidence cannot show

The 9.5% vacant-unit share and 52.2% owner share cannot determine competition for a specific San Antonio, TX listing, seller flexibility, inspection risk, appraisal results, insurance availability, or future supply. Likewise, 612,901 total units say nothing about how many properties fit a borrower's price, location, accessibility, unit-count, or condition requirements.

For the next comparison in San Antonio, TX, the ACS period behind the 9.5% vacancy share can accurately describe the broad stock while lagging a fast-moving listing market. For borrower planning, borrowers should, as a result, keep 58,320 vacant units separate from active inventory and keep the $1,324 median separate from a current lease quote, using each figure only for the question its definition can answer.

Next step: compare current local choices on the same terms

A grounded San Antonio, TX comparison starts with current listings and lease offers, then uses the ACS evidence to check whether the sample is being interpreted in the context of 612,901 total units, a vacancy share of 9.5%, and an owner share of 52.2%.

For borrowers reviewing evidence from San Antonio, TX, on the rental side, replace the $1,324 median with the actual lease, utilities, deposits, and expected changes. On the ownership side, keep the 52.2% share as market context while adding the selected property's price, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, loan disclosures, and cash-to-close. In practical terms, the resulting worksheet will not turn 47.8% or 52.2% into a recommendation, but it will keep the two paths comparable and make the remaining assumptions visible before a decision.

Methodology

This article uses 2024 ACS 5-year place estimates for units, occupancy, vacancy, owner and renter tenure, and gross rent, with 2019 evidence retained for comparison review. Figures are reported as published for each cited geography and period. Derived percentages and differences use only the displayed source values; unavailable figures are omitted rather than estimated.

What this data cannot determine

ACS vacancy includes categories beyond for-sale availability, and tenure and rent estimates carry sampling error and do not measure current listing inventory. The evidence is broad market context, not a property valuation, rate or payment quote, eligibility finding, underwriting decision, product recommendation, or prediction.
