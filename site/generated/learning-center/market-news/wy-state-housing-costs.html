Wyoming housing costs, ownership, and rent in the latest ACS

Statewide evidence on home value, income, owner costs, housing stock, tenure, vacancy, and rent for borrower planning.

Key takeaways

Evidence at a glance: statewide value, income, and owner costs

Start with the dated local finding: Wyoming's 2024 ACS housing baseline reports a median owner-occupied home value of $309,700, median household income of $76,176, and median selected monthly owner costs of $1,754 for owners with a mortgage. Those three reference points may help a borrower identify which statewide value, income, and recurring-cost assumptions need local replacement first. They remain separate medians, not the price, income, and monthly budget of one typical household, and the $1,754 measure is not a real-estate-tax figure or a current mortgage quote. For borrower planning, a real decision still requires local property evidence, borrower documentation, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, and current loan information.

Keep owner costs and rent comparable

The same Wyoming release reports $992 in median gross rent alongside $1,754 in median selected monthly owner costs for owners with a mortgage. The two measures cover different housing populations and cost definitions, so $992 should not be compared with only principal and interest or used to declare a statewide break-even point. A useful comparison replaces the $992 and $1,754 medians with a current lease and a current property budget, including utilities, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, transaction costs, and the expected time horizon. For borrower planning, the $76,176 statewide median can provide economic context, but dividing it by either $992 or $1,754 would still combine medians that do not represent the same household.

Evidence check: how occupied housing is divided by tenure

Across Wyoming's measured housing stock of 277,141 units, the owner share of occupied housing is 71.8% and the renter share is 28.2%, making owner-occupied housing the larger statewide tenure group.

For borrower planning, the 71.8% and 28.2% shares describe how occupied units were distributed during the survey period; they do not measure current buyer demand, owner willingness to sell, or renter readiness to purchase. The 71.8% and 28.2% statewide tenure split is useful for understanding scale and checking assumptions about the housing mix. It remains too broad to select a product or characterize a particular city, county, property type, or household inside Wyoming.

Evidence check: keep the statewide ACS vintages separate

The 2019 ACS published $220,500 as Wyoming's median owner-occupied home-value estimate, while the 2024 ACS published $309,700. In practical terms, these nominal statewide observations may help show why an older planning reference needs current local evidence. They are not inflation-adjusted, and the ACS home-value methodology changed between the vintages, so the two dollar figures are not a like-for-like appreciation series and should not be converted into a precise percentage change.

Applied to a decision in Wyoming, both values summarize multi-year survey windows and carry sampling error. Keep $220,500 attached to 2019 and $309,700 attached to 2024, then use current local and property-specific evidence for purchase, refinance, or equity assumptions.

Evidence check: vacancy is broader than homes for sale

Wyoming's vacant-unit share is 12.2% within a statewide stock of 277,141 housing units.

In a borrower review centered on Wyoming, ACS vacancy includes categories beyond property currently offered for sale or rent, so 12.2% is not a live inventory rate and cannot measure competition for a selected home. Read beside the 71.8% owner share and 28.2% renter share, the vacancy figure helps describe how the broad stock was classified without predicting near-term supply. The borrower should use current listings, property condition, days on market, concessions, and local professional context to answer transaction questions that 12.2% and 277,141 were not designed to resolve.

Next step: move from statewide context to a local worksheet

As local context for Wyoming, borrowers can carry $309,700, $1,754, $992, and the 12.2% vacancy share into a local comparison as dated statewide context, then replace each with evidence matched to the actual decision. On the ownership side, replace the $309,700 and $1,754 medians with a selected price, down payment, current loan disclosures, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, and cash reserves.

For the rental budget, replace the $992 median with a comparable lease, utilities, deposits, and expected changes over the same horizon. The 28.2% renter share is useful for describing the statewide tenure mix, not for predicting which local option will be available. The 71.8% tenure measure may still help explain the statewide housing mix, but it cannot decide whether buying, renting, refinancing, or using equity is suitable for one Wyoming household.

Methodology

This article uses 2024 ACS 5-year state estimates for home value, household income, selected owner costs, rent, total and occupied housing units, vacancy, and owner and renter tenure, plus a separate 2019 nominal home-value observation. It does not calculate a percentage change between home-value vintages. Figures are reported as published for each cited geography and period. Derived percentages and differences use only the displayed source values; unavailable figures are omitted rather than estimated.

What this data cannot determine

ACS estimates cover multi-year survey periods, carry margins of error, and do not measure current listings, current financing terms, or a specific household's housing costs. The 2019 and 2024 home-value dollars are not inflation-adjusted, and their methodology is not directly comparable. The evidence is broad market context, not a property valuation, rate or payment quote, eligibility finding, underwriting decision, product recommendation, or prediction.
