Ohio housing costs, ownership, and rent in the latest ACS

Statewide evidence on home value, income, owner costs, housing stock, tenure, vacancy, and rent for borrower planning.

U.S. Census Bureau

Key takeaways

  • state median home value: $214,800.
  • prior ACS state median home value: $145,700.
  • state median household income: $71,389.
  • median monthly owner cost with a mortgage: $1,517.
  • median gross rent: $1,034.

Statewide value, income, and owner costs

Ohio's 2024 ACS housing baseline reports a median owner-occupied home value of $214,800, median household income of $71,389, and median selected monthly owner costs of $1,517 for owners with a mortgage. The $214,800, $71,389, and $1,517 values are separate statewide medians, so they should not be treated as the price, income, and monthly budget of one typical household. The $1,517 measure combines recurring ownership expenses and is not a real-estate-tax figure or a current mortgage quote. For planning, $214,800, $71,389, and $1,517 identify statewide reference points and cost categories, while a real decision still requires local property, borrower, tax, insurance, and loan information.

Keep owner costs and rent comparable

The same Ohio release reports $1,034 in median gross rent alongside $1,517 in median selected monthly owner costs for owners with a mortgage. The two measures cover different housing populations and cost definitions, so $1,034 should not be compared with only principal and interest or used to declare a statewide break-even point. A useful comparison replaces the $1,034 and $1,517 medians with a current lease and a current property budget, including utilities, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, transaction costs, and the expected time horizon. The $71,389 statewide median can provide economic context, but dividing it by either $1,034 or $1,517 would still combine medians that do not represent the same household.

How occupied housing is divided by tenure

Across Ohio's measured housing stock of 5,292,391 units, the owner share of occupied housing is 67.2% and the renter share is 32.8%, making owner-occupied housing the larger statewide tenure group. The 67.2% and 32.8% shares describe how occupied units were distributed during the survey period; they do not measure current buyer demand, owner willingness to sell, or renter readiness to purchase. The 67.2% and 32.8% statewide tenure split is useful for understanding scale and checking assumptions about the housing mix. It remains too broad to select a product or characterize a particular city, county, property type, or household inside Ohio.

Vacancy is broader than homes for sale

Ohio's vacant-unit share is 8.1% within a statewide stock of 5,292,391 housing units. ACS vacancy includes categories beyond property currently offered for sale or rent, so 8.1% is not a live inventory rate and cannot measure competition for a selected home. Read beside the 67.2% owner share and 32.8% renter share, the vacancy figure helps describe how the broad stock was classified without predicting near-term supply. Borrowers should use current listings, property condition, days on market, concessions, and local professional context to answer transaction questions that 8.1% and 5,292,391 were not designed to resolve.

The statewide home-value estimate changed across periods

Ohio's median home-value estimate increased from $145,700 in the prior ACS period to $214,800 in the current release, a reported change of 47.4%. Because both estimates summarize multi-year survey windows, 47.4% is not a point-to-point appreciation rate for a property purchased at $145,700. The 47.4% comparison can show that an old statewide reference may need updating, but it cannot explain the value path in each local market or price segment. Margins of error, changes in the housing mix, and current property evidence should be reviewed before the move from $145,700 to $214,800 influences a purchase, refinance, or equity assumption.

Move from statewide context to a local worksheet

A borrower can carry $214,800, $1,517, $1,034, and the 8.1% vacancy share into a local comparison as dated statewide context, then replace each with evidence matched to the actual decision. For ownership, replace the $214,800 and $1,517 medians with a selected price, down payment, current loan disclosures, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, and cash reserves. For renting, replace the $1,034 median with a comparable lease, utilities, deposits, and expected changes over the same horizon. The 32.8% renter share is useful for describing the statewide tenure mix, not for predicting which local option will be available. The 67.2% tenure measure can help explain the statewide housing mix, but it cannot decide whether buying, renting, refinancing, or using equity is suitable for one Ohio household.

Methodology

This article uses 2024 ACS 5-year state estimates for home value, household income, selected owner costs, rent, total and occupied housing units, vacancy, and owner and renter tenure, plus a 2019 value comparison. Figures are reported as published for each cited geography and period. Derived percentages and differences use only the displayed source values; unavailable figures are omitted rather than estimated.

What this data cannot determine

ACS estimates cover multi-year survey periods, carry margins of error, and do not measure current listings, current financing terms, or a specific household's housing costs. The evidence is broad market context, not a property valuation, rate or payment quote, eligibility finding, underwriting decision, product recommendation, or prediction.
