Utah housing costs, ownership, and rent in the latest ACS

Statewide evidence on home value, income, owner costs, housing stock, tenure, vacancy, and rent for borrower planning.

The dated finding: statewide value, income, and owner costs

The published evidence provides the starting point: Utah's 2024 ACS housing baseline reports a median owner-occupied home value of $489,400, median household income of $95,166, and median selected monthly owner costs of $2,009 for owners with a mortgage. For the decision at hand, those three reference points may help a borrower identify which statewide value, income, and recurring-cost assumptions need local replacement first. They remain separate medians, not the price, income, and monthly budget of one typical household, and the $2,009 measure is not a real-estate-tax figure or a current mortgage quote. In practical terms, a real decision continues to require local property evidence, borrower documentation, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, and current loan information.

Start with the evidence: keep owner costs and rent comparable

The same Utah release reports $1,496 in median gross rent alongside $2,009 in median selected monthly owner costs for owners with a mortgage. In practical terms, the two measures cover different housing populations and cost definitions, so $1,496 should not be compared with only principal and interest or used to declare a statewide break-even point. A useful comparison replaces the $1,496 and $2,009 medians with a current lease and a current property budget, including utilities, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, transaction costs, and the expected time horizon. For the decision at hand, the $95,166 statewide median can provide economic context, but dividing it by either $1,496 or $2,009 would still combine medians that do not represent the same household.

Planning context: vacancy is broader than homes for sale

Utah's vacant-unit share is 8.1% within a statewide stock of 1,223,468 housing units. In practical terms, ACS vacancy includes categories beyond property currently offered for sale or rent, so 8.1% is not a live inventory rate and cannot measure competition for a selected home.

Applied to a decision in Utah, read beside the 70.2% owner share and 29.8% renter share, the vacancy figure helps describe how the broad stock was classified without predicting near-term supply. For the decision at hand, a borrower should use current listings, property condition, days on market, concessions, and local professional context to answer transaction questions that 8.1% and 1,223,468 were not designed to resolve.

Borrower view: how occupied housing is divided by tenure

Across Utah's measured housing stock of 1,223,468 units, the owner share of occupied housing is 70.2% and the renter share is 29.8%, making owner-occupied housing the larger statewide tenure group. For the decision at hand, the 70.2% and 29.8% shares describe how occupied units were distributed during the survey period; they do not measure current buyer demand, owner willingness to sell, or renter readiness to purchase.

The 70.2% and 29.8% statewide tenure split is useful for understanding scale and checking assumptions about the housing mix. In practical terms, it remains too broad to select a product or characterize a particular city, county, property type, or household inside Utah.

Keep the statewide ACS vintages separate

The 2019 ACS published $279,100 as Utah's median owner-occupied home-value estimate, while the 2024 ACS published $489,400.

For the next comparison in Utah, these nominal statewide observations may still help show why an older planning reference needs current local evidence. They are not inflation-adjusted, and the ACS home-value methodology changed between the vintages, so the two dollar figures are not a like-for-like appreciation series and should not be converted into a precise percentage change. For borrower planning, both values summarize multi-year survey windows and carry sampling error. Keep $279,100 attached to 2019 and $489,400 attached to 2024, then use current local and property-specific evidence for purchase, refinance, or equity assumptions.

Borrower action: move from statewide context to a local worksheet

A borrower may carry $489,400, $2,009, $1,496, and the 8.1% vacancy share into a local comparison as dated statewide context, then replace each with evidence matched to the actual decision. For ownership, replace the $489,400 and $2,009 medians with a selected price, down payment, current loan disclosures, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, and cash reserves. For the rental budget, replace the $1,496 median with a comparable lease, utilities, deposits, and expected changes over the same horizon. In practical terms, the 29.8% renter share is useful for describing the statewide tenure mix, not for predicting which local option will be available. The 70.2% tenure measure may help explain the statewide housing mix, but it cannot decide whether buying, renting, refinancing, or using equity is suitable for one Utah household.

Methodology

This article uses 2024 ACS 5-year state estimates for home value, household income, selected owner costs, rent, total and occupied housing units, vacancy, and owner and renter tenure, plus a separate 2019 nominal home-value observation. It does not calculate a percentage change between home-value vintages. Figures are reported as published for each cited geography and period. Derived percentages and differences use only the displayed source values; unavailable figures are omitted rather than estimated.

What this data cannot determine

ACS estimates cover multi-year survey periods, carry margins of error, and do not measure current listings, current financing terms, or a specific household's housing costs. The 2019 and 2024 home-value dollars are not inflation-adjusted, and their methodology is not directly comparable. The evidence is broad market context, not a property valuation, rate or payment quote, eligibility finding, underwriting decision, product recommendation, or prediction.
