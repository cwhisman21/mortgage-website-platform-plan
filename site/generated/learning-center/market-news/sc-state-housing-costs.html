South Carolina housing costs, ownership, and rent in the latest ACS

Statewide evidence on home value, income, owner costs, housing stock, tenure, vacancy, and rent for borrower planning.

Start with the evidence: statewide value, income, and owner costs

The local figures answer the first question: South Carolina's 2024 ACS housing baseline reports a median owner-occupied home value of $259,000, median household income of $69,324, and median selected monthly owner costs of $1,529 for owners with a mortgage. For borrower planning, those three reference points may help a borrower identify which statewide value, income, and recurring-cost assumptions need local replacement first. They remain separate medians, not the price, income, and monthly budget of one typical household, and the $1,529 measure is not a real-estate-tax figure or a current mortgage quote. A real decision continues to require local property evidence, borrower documentation, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, and current loan information.

Planning context: how occupied housing is divided by tenure

Across South Carolina's measured housing stock of 2,443,039 units, the owner share of occupied housing is 71.9% and the renter share is 28.1%, making owner-occupied housing the larger statewide tenure group. The 71.9% and 28.1% shares describe how occupied units were distributed during the survey period; they do not measure current buyer demand, owner willingness to sell, or renter readiness to purchase.

The 71.9% and 28.1% statewide tenure split is useful for understanding scale and checking assumptions about the housing mix. For borrower planning, it remains too broad to select a product or characterize a particular city, county, property type, or household inside South Carolina.

Start with the evidence: keep owner costs and rent comparable

The same South Carolina release reports $1,180 in median gross rent alongside $1,529 in median selected monthly owner costs for owners with a mortgage.

In a borrower review centered on South Carolina, the two measures cover different housing populations and cost definitions, so $1,180 should not be compared with only principal and interest or used to declare a statewide break-even point. A useful comparison replaces the $1,180 and $1,529 medians with a current lease and a current property budget, including utilities, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, transaction costs, and the expected time horizon. The $69,324 statewide median can provide economic context, but dividing it by either $1,180 or $1,529 would still combine medians that do not represent the same household.

Borrower view: vacancy is broader than homes for sale

South Carolina's vacant-unit share is 13.3% within a statewide stock of 2,443,039 housing units. ACS vacancy includes categories beyond property currently offered for sale or rent, so 13.3% is not a live inventory rate and cannot measure competition for a selected home.

For the next comparison in South Carolina, read beside the 71.9% owner share and 28.1% renter share, the vacancy figure helps describe how the broad stock was classified without predicting near-term supply. For borrower planning, a borrower should use current listings, property condition, days on market, concessions, and local professional context to answer transaction questions that 13.3% and 2,443,039 were not designed to resolve.

Borrower view: keep the statewide ACS vintages separate

The 2019 ACS published $162,300 as South Carolina's median owner-occupied home-value estimate, while the 2024 ACS published $259,000. For the decision at hand, these nominal statewide observations can nevertheless help show why an older planning reference needs current local evidence. They are not inflation-adjusted, and the ACS home-value methodology changed between the vintages, so the two dollar figures are not a like-for-like appreciation series and should not be converted into a precise percentage change. In practical terms, both values summarize multi-year survey windows and carry sampling error. Keep $162,300 attached to 2019 and $259,000 attached to 2024, then use current local and property-specific evidence for purchase, refinance, or equity assumptions.

Move from statewide context to a local worksheet

In borrower planning for South Carolina, borrowers can carry $259,000, $1,529, $1,180, and the 13.3% vacancy share into a local comparison as dated statewide context, then replace each with evidence matched to the actual decision.

On the ownership side, replace the $259,000 and $1,529 medians with a selected price, down payment, current loan disclosures, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, and cash reserves. On the rental side, replace the $1,180 median with a comparable lease, utilities, deposits, and expected changes over the same horizon. For borrower planning, the 28.1% renter share is useful for describing the statewide tenure mix, not for predicting which local option will be available. The 71.9% tenure measure may help explain the statewide housing mix, but it cannot decide whether buying, renting, refinancing, or using equity is suitable for one South Carolina household.

Methodology

This article uses 2024 ACS 5-year state estimates for home value, household income, selected owner costs, rent, total and occupied housing units, vacancy, and owner and renter tenure, plus a separate 2019 nominal home-value observation. It does not calculate a percentage change between home-value vintages. Figures are reported as published for each cited geography and period. Derived percentages and differences use only the displayed source values; unavailable figures are omitted rather than estimated.

What this data cannot determine

ACS estimates cover multi-year survey periods, carry margins of error, and do not measure current listings, current financing terms, or a specific household's housing costs. The 2019 and 2024 home-value dollars are not inflation-adjusted, and their methodology is not directly comparable. The evidence is broad market context, not a property valuation, rate or payment quote, eligibility finding, underwriting decision, product recommendation, or prediction.
