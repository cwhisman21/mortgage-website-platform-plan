Pennsylvania housing costs, ownership, and rent in the latest ACS

Statewide evidence on home value, income, owner costs, housing stock, tenure, vacancy, and rent for borrower planning.

Statewide value, income, and owner costs

Pennsylvania's 2024 ACS housing baseline reports a median owner-occupied home value of $254,500, median household income of $77,971, and median selected monthly owner costs of $1,762 for owners with a mortgage. In practical terms, those three reference points can help a borrower identify which statewide value, income, and recurring-cost assumptions need local replacement first. They remain separate medians, not the price, income, and monthly budget of one typical household, and the $1,762 measure is not a real-estate-tax figure or a current mortgage quote. For the decision at hand, a real decision still needs local property evidence, borrower documentation, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, and current loan information.

Keep owner costs and rent comparable

The same Pennsylvania release reports $1,209 in median gross rent alongside $1,762 in median selected monthly owner costs for owners with a mortgage. For the decision at hand, the two measures cover different housing populations and cost definitions, so $1,209 should not be compared with only principal and interest or used to declare a statewide break-even point.

For borrowers reviewing evidence from Pennsylvania, a useful comparison replaces the $1,209 and $1,762 medians with a current lease and a current property budget, including utilities, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, transaction costs, and the expected time horizon. In practical terms, the $77,971 statewide median can provide economic context, but dividing it by either $1,209 or $1,762 would still combine medians that do not represent the same household.

Evidence check: how occupied housing is divided by tenure

Across Pennsylvania's measured housing stock of 5,806,452 units, the owner share of occupied housing is 69.3% and the renter share is 30.7%, making owner-occupied housing the larger statewide tenure group. In practical terms, the 69.3% and 30.7% shares describe how occupied units were distributed during the survey period; they do not measure current buyer demand, owner willingness to sell, or renter readiness to purchase. The 69.3% and 30.7% statewide tenure split is useful for understanding scale and checking assumptions about the housing mix. For the decision at hand, it remains too broad to select a product or characterize a particular city, county, property type, or household inside Pennsylvania.

Evidence check: vacancy is broader than homes for sale

Pennsylvania's vacant-unit share is 9.2% within a statewide stock of 5,806,452 housing units.

For borrowers reviewing evidence from Pennsylvania, ACS vacancy includes categories beyond property currently offered for sale or rent, so 9.2% is not a live inventory rate and cannot measure competition for a selected home. Read beside the 69.3% owner share and 30.7% renter share, the vacancy figure helps describe how the broad stock was classified without predicting near-term supply. In practical terms, a borrower should use current listings, property condition, days on market, concessions, and local professional context to answer transaction questions that 9.2% and 5,806,452 were not designed to resolve.

Borrower view: keep the statewide ACS vintages separate

The 2019 ACS published $180,200 as Pennsylvania's median owner-occupied home-value estimate, while the 2024 ACS published $254,500. For borrower planning, these nominal statewide observations can still help show why an older planning reference needs current local evidence.

In a borrower review centered on Pennsylvania, they are not inflation-adjusted, and the ACS home-value methodology changed between the vintages, so the two dollar figures are not a like-for-like appreciation series and should not be converted into a precise percentage change. Both values summarize multi-year survey windows and carry sampling error. Keep $180,200 attached to 2019 and $254,500 attached to 2024, then use current local and property-specific evidence for purchase, refinance, or equity assumptions.

Next step: move from statewide context to a local worksheet

For a decision in Pennsylvania, borrowers can carry $254,500, $1,762, $1,209, and the 9.2% vacancy share into a local comparison as dated statewide context, then replace each with evidence matched to the actual decision. On the ownership side, replace the $254,500 and $1,762 medians with a selected price, down payment, current loan disclosures, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, and cash reserves. For the rental budget, replace the $1,209 median with a comparable lease, utilities, deposits, and expected changes over the same horizon.

For the decision at hand, the 30.7% renter share is useful for describing the statewide tenure mix, not for predicting which local option will be available. The 69.3% tenure measure may help explain the statewide housing mix, but it cannot decide whether buying, renting, refinancing, or using equity is suitable for one Pennsylvania household.

Methodology

This article uses 2024 ACS 5-year state estimates for home value, household income, selected owner costs, rent, total and occupied housing units, vacancy, and owner and renter tenure, plus a separate 2019 nominal home-value observation. It does not calculate a percentage change between home-value vintages. Figures are reported as published for each cited geography and period. Derived percentages and differences use only the displayed source values; unavailable figures are omitted rather than estimated.

What this data cannot determine

ACS estimates cover multi-year survey periods, carry margins of error, and do not measure current listings, current financing terms, or a specific household's housing costs. The 2019 and 2024 home-value dollars are not inflation-adjusted, and their methodology is not directly comparable. The evidence is broad market context, not a property valuation, rate or payment quote, eligibility finding, underwriting decision, product recommendation, or prediction.
