Denver, CO housing supply, ownership, and rent

What the latest ACS housing mix can and cannot tell buyers and renters comparing options in Denver, CO.

Maya Brooks Snap Mortgage Editorial Contributor Last updated July 10, 2026 Snap editorial voice, not a loan officer or licensed mortgage professional.

U.S. Census Bureau

Key takeaways

  • As of: 2024 ACS 5-year estimates. Source period: 2024.
  • total housing units: 359,581.
  • occupied housing units: 335,428.
  • vacant housing units: 24,153.

Evidence at a glance: the size and use of the local housing stock

Start with the dated local finding: Denver, CO's current ACS housing baseline includes 359,581 total housing units, 335,428 occupied units, and 24,153 vacant units. In practical terms, those counts may help a borrower establish the scale of the measured housing base before comparing tenure, rent, current listings, property condition, and financing. The figures describe stock across the survey period; they do not say that 24,153 homes were listed for sale or available to rent at one moment. For the decision at hand, reading 359,581 total units with 335,428 occupied and 24,153 vacant units keeps the release internally grounded while leaving live availability to current listing and property evidence.

Borrower view: use rent as a matched comparison, not a shortcut

Denver, CO's ACS median gross rent is $1,831, while renter-occupied units account for 51.2% of occupied housing and owner-occupied units account for 48.8%.

The $1,831 median includes rent and certain utilities for the measured rental stock; it cannot reveal a lease offer comparable to a home under consideration. Comparing $1,831 with only a mortgage's principal and interest would omit taxes, insurance, maintenance, association charges, transaction costs, and differences between the properties. For borrower planning, a more useful Denver, CO comparison matches real lease and ownership choices over the same expected horizon, then treats the tenure shares as market context instead of a break-even calculation.

Read the comparison: vacancy changed, but it is not listing inventory

Vacant units in Denver, CO increased from 20,216 in the prior ACS period to 24,153 in the current release, a reported movement of 19.5%. In the current housing stock, 24,153 units equal 6.7% of the 359,581 total.

ACS vacancy covers several statuses, so the 6.7% share cannot be read as the share of homes a buyer can purchase today or the share of rentals immediately available. For borrower planning, the movement from 20,216 to 24,153 may still prompt better questions about current for-sale and rental inventory in Denver, CO, provided those questions are answered with timely listing and property data rather than the survey count alone.

Evidence check: ownership and renting within occupied housing

Among 335,428 occupied units in Denver, CO, the ACS-derived owner share is 48.8% and the renter share is 51.2%, making renter-occupied housing the larger measured tenure group. For the decision at hand, the 48.8% and 51.2% figures describe how occupied units were distributed; they do not measure how many households are ready to buy, how many owners plan to sell, or which properties can qualify for a mortgage.

This local tenure mix helps a borrower avoid assuming that one housing path dominates every segment of Denver, CO. In practical terms, the 48.8% and 51.2% mix is most useful when paired with actual choices at a similar property type, location, condition, and time horizon rather than used as a signal to buy or rent by itself.

What the housing-stock evidence cannot show

The 6.7% vacant-unit share and 48.8% owner share cannot decide competition for a specific Denver, CO listing, seller flexibility, inspection risk, appraisal results, insurance availability, or future supply. For the decision at hand, likewise, 359,581 total units say nothing about how many properties fit a borrower's price, location, accessibility, unit-count, or condition requirements.

For borrowers reviewing evidence from Denver, CO, the ACS period behind the 6.7% vacancy share can accurately describe the broad stock while lagging a fast-moving listing market. In practical terms, the borrower therefore should keep 24,153 vacant units separate from active inventory and keep the $1,831 median separate from a current lease quote, using each figure only for the question its definition can answer.

Next step: compare current local choices on the same terms

A grounded Denver, CO comparison starts with current listings and lease offers, then uses the ACS evidence to check whether the sample is being interpreted in the context of 359,581 total units, a vacancy share of 6.7%, and an owner share of 48.8%. On the rental side, replace the $1,831 median with the actual lease, utilities, deposits, and expected changes.

For the next comparison in Denver, CO, for ownership, keep the 48.8% share as market context while adding the selected property's price, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, loan disclosures, and cash-to-close. For borrower planning, the resulting worksheet will not turn 51.2% or 48.8% into a recommendation, but it will keep the two paths comparable and make the remaining assumptions visible before a decision.

Methodology

This article uses 2024 ACS 5-year place estimates for units, occupancy, vacancy, owner and renter tenure, and gross rent, with 2019 evidence retained for comparison review. Figures are reported as published for each cited geography and period. Derived percentages and differences use only the displayed source values; unavailable figures are omitted rather than estimated.

What this data cannot determine

ACS vacancy includes categories beyond for-sale availability, and tenure and rent estimates carry sampling error and do not measure current listing inventory. The evidence is broad market context, not a property valuation, rate or payment quote, eligibility finding, underwriting decision, product recommendation, or prediction.
