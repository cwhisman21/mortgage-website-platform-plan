Start with the evidence: the conforming range across county records

The local figures answer the first question: Iowa's statewide summary covers 99 county or county-equivalent records. Within those records, the 2026 one-unit conforming limit runs from $832,750 to $832,750. That range can help borrowers see why the property county must be identified before a loan amount is assigned a conforming or jumbo category. For borrower planning, the matched records share a one-unit conforming figure of $832,750, but the applicable county row still must be verified for a property. The 99 record count keeps the range tied to the statewide source coverage rather than an assumed national rule. The $832,750 to $832,750 amounts classify loan size within the published program table; they do not establish a borrower's capacity, a property's value, or the price of financing. Before using the $832,750 statewide maximum, county, legal unit count, and expected base loan amount must be known.

The FHA range follows its own county table

Across the same 99 Iowa records, the 2026 one-unit FHA limit spans $541,287 to $541,287. In practical terms, the matched FHA rows share $541,287, although property geography and current program guidance still require confirmation. The $541,287 floor and $541,287 high remain county-table values even when they match conforming amounts elsewhere.

Applied to a decision in Iowa, the FHA range should not be merged with the $832,750 to $832,750 conforming range, because the programs use different authorities and requirements. A $541,287 to $541,287 comparison can route loan-amount and product-category questions, but it does not determine FHA eligibility, mortgage insurance, appraisal acceptability, occupancy compliance, cash needs, or approval.

A larger county limit is not greater affordability

A $832,750 conforming ceiling or $541,287 FHA ceiling does not make that loan amount affordable to a household in Iowa, and the lower $832,750 or $541,287 figures do not describe home prices. In practical terms, published $832,750 and $541,287 limits cannot prove borrower income, debts, credit, assets, occupancy, property condition, appraisal, insurance, reserves, rates, fees, or final approval.

For the decision at hand, keeping the 99 county-record scope and 2026 period attached to the figures prevents the statewide summary from being mistaken for a personalized product recommendation or a single amount that applies everywhere.

Methodology

This article joins official 2026 FHFA conforming and HUD FHA county files by five-digit county FIPS, then summarizes the observed one-unit range and county counts for the state. Figures are reported as published for each cited geography and period. Derived percentages and differences use only the displayed source values; unavailable figures are omitted rather than estimated.

What this data cannot determine

State summaries can conceal special exceptions and multi-unit differences, and official program updates or property-location corrections can change the applicable row. The evidence is broad market context, not a property valuation, rate or payment quote, eligibility finding, underwriting decision, product recommendation, or prediction.
