California housing costs, ownership, and rent in the latest ACS

Statewide evidence on home value, income, owner costs, housing stock, tenure, vacancy, and rent for borrower planning.

Maya Brooks Snap Mortgage Editorial Contributor Last updated July 10, 2026 Snap editorial voice, not a loan officer or licensed mortgage professional.

Key takeaways

  • As of: 2024 ACS 5-year estimates. Source period: 2024.
  • state median home value: $734,700.
  • prior ACS state median home value: $505,000.
  • state median household income: $99,122.

Evidence at a glance: statewide value, income, and owner costs

Start with the dated local finding: California's 2024 ACS housing baseline reports a median owner-occupied home value of $734,700, median household income of $99,122, and median selected monthly owner costs of $2,946 for owners with a mortgage. Those three reference points can still help a borrower identify which statewide value, income, and recurring-cost assumptions need local replacement first. They remain separate medians, not the price, income, and monthly budget of one typical household, and the $2,946 measure is not a real-estate-tax figure or a current mortgage quote. For borrower planning, a real decision still needs local property evidence, borrower documentation, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, and current loan information.

Evidence at a glance: keep owner costs and rent comparable

The same California release reports $2,036 in median gross rent alongside $2,946 in median selected monthly owner costs for owners with a mortgage. The two measures cover different housing populations and cost definitions, so $2,036 should not be compared with only principal and interest or used to declare a statewide break-even point.

For the next comparison in California, a useful comparison replaces the $2,036 and $2,946 medians with a current lease and a current property budget, including utilities, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, transaction costs, and the expected time horizon. For borrower planning, the $99,122 statewide median can provide economic context, but dividing it by either $2,036 or $2,946 would still combine medians that do not represent the same household.

Evidence check: how occupied housing is divided by tenure

Across California's measured housing stock of 14,644,735 units, the owner share of occupied housing is 55.9% and the renter share is 44.1%, making owner-occupied housing the larger statewide tenure group.

For borrower planning, the 55.9% and 44.1% shares describe how occupied units were distributed during the survey period; they do not measure current buyer demand, owner willingness to sell, or renter readiness to purchase. The 55.9% and 44.1% statewide tenure split is useful for understanding scale and checking assumptions about the housing mix. It remains too broad to select a product or characterize a particular city, county, property type, or household inside California.

Planning context: keep the statewide ACS vintages separate

The 2019 ACS published $505,000 as California's median owner-occupied home-value estimate, while the 2024 ACS published $734,700. In practical terms, these nominal statewide observations may help show why an older planning reference needs current local evidence. They are not inflation-adjusted, and the ACS home-value methodology changed between the vintages, so the two dollar figures are not a like-for-like appreciation series and should not be converted into a precise percentage change.

Applied to a decision in California, both values summarize multi-year survey windows and carry sampling error. Keep $505,000 attached to 2019 and $734,700 attached to 2024, then use current local and property-specific evidence for purchase, refinance, or equity assumptions.

Vacancy is broader than homes for sale

California's vacant-unit share is 7.5% within a statewide stock of 14,644,735 housing units. For borrower planning, ACS vacancy includes categories beyond property currently offered for sale or rent, so 7.5% is not a live inventory rate and cannot measure competition for a selected home.

In a borrower review centered on California, read beside the 55.9% owner share and 44.1% renter share, the vacancy figure helps describe how the broad stock was classified without predicting near-term supply. Borrowers should use current listings, property condition, days on market, concessions, and local professional context to answer transaction questions that 7.5% and 14,644,735 were not designed to resolve.

Move from statewide context to a local worksheet

As local context for California, a borrower can carry $734,700, $2,946, $2,036, and the 7.5% vacancy share into a local comparison as dated statewide context, then replace each with evidence matched to the actual decision. For the ownership budget, replace the $734,700 and $2,946 medians with a selected price, down payment, current loan disclosures, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, and cash reserves.

For the rental budget, replace the $2,036 median with a comparable lease, utilities, deposits, and expected changes over the same horizon. The 44.1% renter share is useful for describing the statewide tenure mix, not for predicting which local option will be available. The 55.9% tenure measure can nevertheless help explain the statewide housing mix, but it cannot decide whether buying, renting, refinancing, or using equity is suitable for one California household.

Methodology

This article uses 2024 ACS 5-year state estimates for home value, household income, selected owner costs, rent, total and occupied housing units, vacancy, and owner and renter tenure, plus a separate 2019 nominal home-value observation. It does not calculate a percentage change between home-value vintages. Figures are reported as published for each cited geography and period. Derived percentages and differences use only the displayed source values; unavailable figures are omitted rather than estimated.

What this data cannot determine

ACS estimates cover multi-year survey periods, carry margins of error, and do not measure current listings, current financing terms, or a specific household's housing costs. The 2019 and 2024 home-value dollars are not inflation-adjusted, and their methodology is not directly comparable. The evidence is broad market context, not a property valuation, rate or payment quote, eligibility finding, underwriting decision, product recommendation, or prediction.
