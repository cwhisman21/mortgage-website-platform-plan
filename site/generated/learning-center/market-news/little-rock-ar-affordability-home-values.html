The local value, income, and owner-cost baseline

In Little Rock, AR, the 2024 ACS reports a median owner-occupied home value of $236,400 and median household income of $63,003; these are separate medians, not the finances of one representative household. The same release reports $1,646 in median selected monthly owner costs for owners with a mortgage, a measure that combines recurring ownership expenses rather than real estate taxes alone. For Little Rock, AR, reading $236,400, $63,003, and $1,646 together identifies the cost categories that deserve verification without converting public medians into a payment quote. An affordability review can start with the local $236,400, $63,003, and $1,646 reference points, then replace them with the borrower's income, cash, debts, property taxes, insurance, association charges, loan terms, and intended occupancy.

What changed between the ACS periods

Little Rock, AR's median home-value estimate increased from $167,600 in the prior ACS period to $236,400 in the current release, a reported change of 41.1%. Because each ACS 5-year estimate summarizes a multi-year survey window, the 41.1% comparison describes a change between published estimates rather than appreciation for a particular home. The move from $167,600 to $236,400 can help a borrower test whether an older search budget still reflects the broad local evidence, but it cannot identify a current asking price or future resale value in Little Rock, AR. Before the 41.1% change is treated as precise, the analysis still needs margins of error, property type, neighborhood, condition, and the timing of an actual transaction.

How the city estimate sits within the state

Little Rock, AR's $236,400 median home-value estimate is above the statewide $188,000 estimate, with a reported city-to-state gap of 25.7%. That 25.7% gap is useful for showing that a statewide midpoint may be a poor substitute for a local search range in Little Rock, AR. It does not mean every local property is 25.7% away from a comparable property elsewhere in the state, because the medians reflect different housing mixes and geographies. A borrower can use $236,400 and $188,000 to decide which assumptions need local replacement first, then compare actual properties and current financing disclosures on the same basis.

What the owner-cost estimate includes and excludes

The $1,646 owner-cost estimate gives Little Rock, AR borrowers a broader monthly reference than principal and interest alone, while the $63,003 income estimate supplies separate household context. The two medians should not be divided to claim that a typical household spends a particular share of income, because ACS does not say the household at $63,003 is the owner household at $1,646. The $236,400 local home-value estimate adds market context, but it also cannot supply a down payment, interest rate, mortgage insurance amount, tax bill, or insurance quote. Keeping $236,400, $63,003, and $1,646 in their published roles prevents a local affordability worksheet from acquiring false precision before a property and borrower are known.

What these local medians cannot decide

Neither $236,400 nor $1,646 determines the value of a specific Little Rock, AR home, what a borrower will pay each month, or an underwriting result. The $63,003 median describes the local household distribution, not the stability, continuity, documentation, or eligible amount of an applicant's income. Even the observed 41.1% between ACS periods cannot forecast the next market move or establish a safe purchase horizon. Those boundaries keep the accurate $236,400, $1,646, and $63,003 statistics from being misused as an appraisal, rate quote, underwriting result, or recommendation about how much debt one household should carry.

Build a property-specific comparison

A practical Little Rock, AR worksheet can begin with $236,400 as broad value context, $1,646 as a reminder to include recurring ownership expenses, and $63,003 as a local economic reference. After noting those $236,400, $1,646, and $63,003 medians, substitute a real property price, down-payment plan, current loan disclosures, taxes, insurance, association obligations, maintenance allowance, and cash reserves. The 25.7% city-to-state difference is a prompt to keep the analysis local, while the 41.1% period change is a prompt to date every assumption. A licensed professional can use the 25.7% and 41.1% comparisons to explain which inputs are verified, which may change, and which loan options fit the documented borrower and property without presenting the ACS figures as an outcome.

Methodology

This article uses 2024 and 2019 ACS 5-year estimates for the Census place and a 2024 parent-state comparison, including published margins of error. Figures are reported as published for each cited geography and period. Derived percentages and differences use only the displayed source values; unavailable figures are omitted rather than estimated.

What this data cannot determine

ACS estimates represent survey periods rather than point-in-time prices, and apparent changes may not be statistically meaningful without a margin-of-error test. The evidence is broad market context, not a property valuation, rate or payment quote, eligibility finding, underwriting decision, product recommendation, or prediction.
