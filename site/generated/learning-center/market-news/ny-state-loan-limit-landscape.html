How conforming and FHA one-unit limits vary across New York counties and how borrowers can use them responsibly.

The conforming range across county records

New York's statewide summary covers 62 county or county-equivalent records. In practical terms, within those records, the 2026 one-unit conforming limit runs from $832,750 to $1,209,750. That range may still help borrowers see why the property county must be identified before a loan amount is assigned a conforming or jumbo category. For the decision at hand, the $832,750 to $1,209,750 range confirms that one conforming ceiling does not apply uniformly across New York. The 62 record count keeps the range tied to the statewide source coverage in place of an assumed national rule. The $832,750 to $1,209,750 amounts classify loan size within the published program table; they do not establish a borrower's capacity, a property's value, or the price of financing. Before using the $1,209,750 statewide maximum, county, legal unit count, and expected base loan amount must be known.

The dated finding: why county verification matters

10 New York counties sit above the conforming baseline in this summary, while the full conforming range reaches from $832,750 to $1,209,750. In practical terms, that count shows where a statewide shortcut can fail: a property in a higher-limit county may use a different published ceiling than one at $832,750. The 10 figure does not mean those counties are more affordable, more desirable, or more likely to approve a loan.

For the decision at hand, it only describes how many matched county rows exceed the baseline. The $1,209,750 maximum should therefore be treated as evidence that county lookup matters, not as the default New York limit. Before treating $1,209,750 as relevant, a borrower should identify the property's official county or county-equivalent area and then attach conventional, jumbo, or FHA labels to the desired loan amount.

Comparison view: conforming and FHA ranges answer different questions

The highest one-unit conforming amount in New York is $1,209,750, while the highest FHA amount is $1,249,125; at the low end, the figures are $832,750 and $541,287. For borrower planning, these pairs show program boundaries, not competing offers.

In a borrower review centered on New York, a requested amount below $1,209,750 may still fall outside the applicable county's conforming limit, and an amount below $1,249,125 may still face FHA borrower and property requirements. Comparing $832,750, $1,209,750, $541,287, and $1,249,125 is useful only when county and unit count are held constant. The $1,209,750 and $1,249,125 ceilings leave rates, fees, mortgage insurance, appraisal, reserves, debt capacity, and product suitability as separate questions for the documented transaction.

The dated finding: the FHA range follows its own county table

Across the same 62 New York records, the 2026 one-unit FHA limit spans $541,287 to $1,249,125. For borrower planning, the $541,287 low and $1,249,125 high show that FHA's published boundary also varies within the state.

In a borrower review centered on New York, the $541,287 floor and $1,249,125 high remain county-table values even when they match conforming amounts elsewhere. The FHA range should not be merged with the $832,750 to $1,209,750 conforming range, because the programs use different authorities and requirements. A $541,287 to $1,249,125 comparison can route loan-amount and product-category questions, but it cannot decide FHA eligibility, mortgage insurance, appraisal acceptability, occupancy compliance, cash needs, or approval.

A larger county limit is not greater affordability

A $1,209,750 conforming ceiling or $1,249,125 FHA ceiling does not make that loan amount affordable to a household in New York, and the lower $832,750 or $541,287 figures do not describe home prices. Published $1,209,750 and $1,249,125 limits cannot prove borrower income, debts, credit, assets, occupancy, property condition, appraisal, insurance, reserves, rates, fees, or final approval.

For the next comparison in New York, the $1,209,750 and $1,249,125 figures can also change with a new calendar year or corrected property geography. For borrower planning, keeping the 62 county-record scope and 2026 period attached to the figures prevents the statewide summary from being mistaken for a personalized product recommendation or a single amount that applies everywhere.

Methodology

This article joins official 2026 FHFA conforming and HUD FHA county files by five-digit county FIPS, then summarizes the observed one-unit range and county counts for the state. Figures are reported as published for each cited geography and period. Derived percentages and differences use only the displayed source values; unavailable figures are omitted rather than estimated.

What this data cannot determine

State summaries can conceal special exceptions and multi-unit differences, and official program updates or property-location corrections can change the applicable row. The evidence is broad market context, not a property valuation, rate or payment quote, eligibility finding, underwriting decision, product recommendation, or prediction.
