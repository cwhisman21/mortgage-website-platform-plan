The local value, income, and owner-cost baseline

In Long Beach, CA, the 2024 ACS reports a median owner-occupied home value of $806,600 and median household income of $87,430; these are separate medians, not the finances of one representative household. The same release reports $2,984 in median selected monthly owner costs for owners with a mortgage, a measure that combines recurring ownership expenses rather than real estate taxes alone. For Long Beach, CA, reading $806,600, $87,430, and $2,984 together identifies the cost categories that deserve verification without converting public medians into a payment quote. An affordability review can start with the local $806,600, $87,430, and $2,984 reference points, then replace them with the borrower's income, cash, debts, property taxes, insurance, association charges, loan terms, and intended occupancy.

What changed between the ACS periods

Long Beach, CA's median home-value estimate increased from $556,100 in the prior ACS period to $806,600 in the current release, a reported change of 45.0%. Because each ACS 5-year estimate summarizes a multi-year survey window, the 45.0% comparison describes a change between published estimates rather than appreciation for a particular home. The move from $556,100 to $806,600 can help a borrower test whether an older search budget still reflects the broad local evidence, but it cannot identify a current asking price or future resale value in Long Beach, CA. Before the 45.0% change is treated as precise, the analysis still needs margins of error, property type, neighborhood, condition, and the timing of an actual transaction.

How the city estimate sits within the state

Long Beach, CA's $806,600 median home-value estimate is above the statewide $734,700 estimate, with a reported city-to-state gap of 9.8%. That 9.8% gap is useful for showing that a statewide midpoint may be a poor substitute for a local search range in Long Beach, CA. It does not mean every local property is 9.8% away from a comparable property elsewhere in the state, because the medians reflect different housing mixes and geographies. A borrower can use $806,600 and $734,700 to decide which assumptions need local replacement first, then compare actual properties and current financing disclosures on the same basis.

What the owner-cost estimate includes and excludes

The $2,984 owner-cost estimate gives Long Beach, CA borrowers a broader monthly reference than principal and interest alone, while the $87,430 income estimate supplies separate household context. The two medians should not be divided to claim that a typical household spends a particular share of income, because ACS does not say the household at $87,430 is the owner household at $2,984. The $806,600 local home-value estimate adds market context, but it also cannot supply a down payment, interest rate, mortgage insurance amount, tax bill, or insurance quote. Keeping $806,600, $87,430, and $2,984 in their published roles prevents a local affordability worksheet from acquiring false precision before a property and borrower are known.

What these local medians cannot decide

Neither $806,600 nor $2,984 determines the value of a specific Long Beach, CA home, what a borrower will pay each month, or an underwriting result. The $87,430 median describes the local household distribution, not the stability, continuity, documentation, or eligible amount of an applicant's income. Even the observed 45.0% between ACS periods cannot forecast the next market move or establish a safe purchase horizon. Those boundaries keep the accurate $806,600, $2,984, and $87,430 statistics from being misused as an appraisal, rate quote, underwriting result, or recommendation about how much debt one household should carry.

Build a property-specific comparison

A practical Long Beach, CA worksheet can begin with $806,600 as broad value context, $2,984 as a reminder to include recurring ownership expenses, and $87,430 as a local economic reference. After noting those $806,600, $2,984, and $87,430 medians, substitute a real property price, down-payment plan, current loan disclosures, taxes, insurance, association obligations, maintenance allowance, and cash reserves. The 9.8% city-to-state difference is a prompt to keep the analysis local, while the 45.0% period change is a prompt to date every assumption. A licensed professional can use the 9.8% and 45.0% comparisons to explain which inputs are verified, which may change, and which loan options fit the documented borrower and property without presenting the ACS figures as an outcome.

Methodology

This article uses 2024 and 2019 ACS 5-year estimates for the Census place and a 2024 parent-state comparison, including published margins of error. Figures are reported as published for each cited geography and period. Derived percentages and differences use only the displayed source values; unavailable figures are omitted rather than estimated.

What this data cannot determine

ACS estimates represent survey periods rather than point-in-time prices, and apparent changes may not be statistically meaningful without a margin-of-error test. The evidence is broad market context, not a property valuation, rate or payment quote, eligibility finding, underwriting decision, product recommendation, or prediction.
