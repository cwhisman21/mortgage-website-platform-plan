Charleston, SC housing supply, ownership, and rent

What the latest ACS housing mix can and cannot tell buyers and renters comparing options in Charleston, SC.

Maya Brooks Snap Mortgage Editorial Contributor Last updated July 10, 2026

U.S. Census Bureau

Key takeaways

  • total housing units: 77,417.
  • occupied housing units: 68,027.
  • vacant housing units: 9,390.
  • prior ACS vacant housing units: 8,121.
  • vacant-unit share of housing stock: 12.1%.

The size and use of the local housing stock

Charleston, SC's current ACS housing baseline includes 77,417 total housing units, 68,027 occupied units, and 9,390 vacant units. Those counts describe the housing stock across the survey period; they do not say that 9,390 homes were listed for sale or available to rent at one moment. The difference between 77,417 total units and 68,027 occupied units is useful for checking the internal scale of the release, while 9,390 provides the numerator for a more careful vacancy reading. For a borrower researching Charleston, SC, the counts establish how large the measured housing base is before tenure, rent, current listings, property condition, and financing questions are added.

Vacancy changed, but it is not listing inventory

Vacant units in Charleston, SC increased from 8,121 in the prior ACS period to 9,390 in the current release, a reported change of 15.6%. In the current housing stock, 9,390 units equal 12.1% of the 77,417 total. ACS vacancy covers several statuses, so the 12.1% share cannot be read as the share of homes a buyer can purchase today or the share of rentals immediately available. The movement from 8,121 to 9,390 can still prompt better questions about current for-sale and rental inventory in Charleston, SC, provided those questions are answered with timely listing and property data rather than the survey count alone.

Ownership and renting within occupied housing

Among 68,027 occupied units in Charleston, SC, the ACS-derived owner share is 55.6% and the renter share is 44.4%, making owner-occupied housing the larger measured tenure group. The 55.6% and 44.4% figures describe how occupied units were distributed; they do not measure how many households are ready to buy, how many owners plan to sell, or which properties can qualify for a mortgage. This local tenure mix helps a borrower avoid assuming that one housing path dominates every segment of Charleston, SC. The 55.6% and 44.4% mix is most useful when paired with actual choices at a similar property type, location, condition, and time horizon rather than used as a signal to buy or rent by itself.

Use rent as a matched comparison, not a shortcut

Charleston, SC's ACS median gross rent is $1,722, while renter-occupied units account for 44.4% of occupied housing and owner-occupied units account for 55.6%. The $1,722 median includes rent and certain utilities for the measured rental stock; it does not identify a lease offer comparable to a home under consideration. Comparing $1,722 with only a mortgage's principal and interest would omit taxes, insurance, maintenance, association charges, transaction costs, and differences between the properties. A more useful Charleston, SC comparison matches real lease and ownership choices over the same expected horizon, then treats the tenure shares as market context instead of a break-even calculation.

What the housing-stock evidence cannot show

The 12.1% vacant-unit share and 55.6% owner share cannot determine competition for a specific Charleston, SC listing, seller flexibility, inspection risk, appraisal results, insurance availability, or future supply. Likewise, 77,417 total units say nothing about how many properties fit a borrower's price, location, accessibility, unit-count, or condition requirements. The ACS period behind the 12.1% vacancy share can accurately describe the broad stock while lagging a fast-moving listing market. Borrowers should therefore keep 9,390 vacant units separate from active inventory and keep the $1,722 median separate from a current lease quote, using each figure only for the question its definition can answer.

Compare current local choices on the same terms

A grounded Charleston, SC comparison starts with current listings and lease offers, then uses the ACS evidence to check whether the sample is being interpreted in the context of 77,417 total units, a 12.1% vacancy share, and a 55.6% owner share. For renting, replace the $1,722 median with the actual lease, utilities, deposits, and expected changes. For ownership, keep the 55.6% share as market context while adding the selected property's price, taxes, insurance, maintenance, association obligations, loan disclosures, and cash-to-close. The resulting worksheet will not turn 44.4% or 55.6% into a recommendation, but it will keep the two paths comparable and make the remaining assumptions visible before a decision.

Methodology

This article uses 2024 ACS 5-year place estimates for units, occupancy, vacancy, owner and renter tenure, and gross rent, with 2019 evidence retained for comparison review. Figures are reported as published for each cited geography and period. Derived percentages and differences use only the displayed source values; unavailable figures are omitted rather than estimated.

What this data cannot determine

ACS vacancy includes categories beyond for-sale availability, and tenure and rent estimates carry sampling error and do not measure current listing inventory. The evidence is broad market context, not a property valuation, rate or payment quote, eligibility finding, underwriting decision, product recommendation, or prediction.
